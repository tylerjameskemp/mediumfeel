What If You Could Build Your Own Thinking Tools?

Chat interfaces give you answers. Building gives you agency.

Exploring · February 10, 2026 · 4 min read 𝕏 in ✉

For eighteen months, I tried countless AI tools: Granola, Otter, Wispr Flow, Fathom, and Fireflies for meeting notes and transcription; Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity on the model front; Make and Zapier to connect data and build automations. I'm a PM and brand strategist, drawn to anything that might sharpen how I think through problems, and the temptation is always to take technology's bait, chasing features instead of focusing on the problems that undergird them.

The pattern was always the same: features that came so close to what I needed, yet never quite right. Tools that didn't connect. Manual steps bridging the gaps. Friction everywhere.

Chat interfaces are fundamentally limiting. You type a question, get an answer, iterate a few times, but you can't break free. You can't reshape how the technology works. You have no agency in how it's deployed. And product often pulls one over on us—we get sold a solution that doesn't address the problem we're actually trying to solve. A distraction dressed as progress.

A Different Kind of Shift

Then I tried Claude Code. Within 48 hours, everything changed.

Not a GUI hiding complexity nor a visual builder with abstractions that break, but a capable assistant in the terminal that could write code, explain decisions, debug with me, and help me learn as I built. The shift wasn't learning a new tool—it was learning to think through problems in a way that lets me build the solutions.

This is the paradigm change: as a problem solver, I can now describe exactly what I need and build the custom-tailored solution myself. Not wait for a product team to prioritize my use case. Not cobble together workarounds. Build.

For the first time in eighteen months of AI hype, that felt possible. Not "someday possible." Not "if I learn to code properly first." Possible now. Now the work is integrating that into how I think: rewiring my problem-solving instincts to include "I could build this" as a real option.

The Problem I Finally Addressed

I have years of data from my practice that's completely under-leveraged: client transcripts, strategy sessions, meeting notes, research, braindumps. Years of thinking sitting in folders, disconnected and underutilized.

I kept asking myself: what would it look like to build something that helps me actually use all this? Not a universal solution, but something specific to how I work. A personal knowledge system that tracks how my thinking evolves.

So I built it.

I asked Claude how to scope a proof of concept, and it suggested two weeks. I built it in two days. The framework mattered: daily standups with myself, explicit "done" criteria for each piece, time-boxed experiments. The technology wasn't just executing tasks; it was helping me manage my own process.

I learned about RAG by implementing it, embeddings by generating them, concept extraction by writing prompts and seeing what broke. Not by reading documentation, but by doing, with a capable partner explaining what I was creating along the way.

The proof of concept (I'm calling it Weave) extracts concepts from documents and links similar ideas across files. It's rough: the transcript extraction is hilariously bad, and the similarity matching needs tuning. Still, it works well enough to explore what I was actually curious about.

The Real Shift

This isn't about the technology. It's never about the technology.

It's about getting better at identifying the problem I'm trying to solve, learning to ask "What tool would solve this?", and then having the agency to build it. The constraint that defined my career—the gap between identifying a problem and having the means to solve it—is collapsing.

Now I'm learning to close that gap myself.
