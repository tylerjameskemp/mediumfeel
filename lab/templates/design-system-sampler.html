What If You Could Build Your Own Thinking Tools?

Exploring · February 5, 2026 · 7 min read · 𝕏 in ✉

After 18 months of AI hype, building something I actually needed finally felt possible. How Claude Code and Opus 4.5 collapsed the distance between "I need a tool" and "here's a working prototype."

← Back to Lab
Type Scale

1.25 ratio. Three voices: Instrument Serif (display), Inter (body), JetBrains Mono (labels).

Post Title — 2.75rem serif

Subsection Label — 1.25rem serif italic

Section Label — 0.875rem mono

Body text at 1.0625rem (17px). Roughly 66 characters per line at 640px — the ideal range for sustained reading. Line-height 1.7 gives comfortable rhythm without feeling loose.

Caption / metadata — 0.875rem (14px)

Footnotes / fine print — 0.75rem (12px)

Body Text & Paragraph Spacing

Paragraph margin: 1.5em (~25px). Snaps to 8px grid. Proportional — scales if font size changes.

The Brief

I took a few of these brain dumps, along with my resume, and pointed Claude at my previous Squarespace site. I asked it to digest everything and help me build something I could host for free elsewhere.

What it came back with, hosted locally on my computer, blew me away. The site is a single HTML file. No frameworks, no build tools. The visual assets are all AI-generated placeholders.

And here we are. The MVP is live. A transitional site, a kernel, something to build on. The shape, feel, and function will all grow and change while amassing documentation.

Bold & Italic

Bold is weight 600 (semi-bold), not 700. Prevents bold from competing with headings. Italic inherits body color — never muted.

Regular text, then bold text at weight 600, then regular again. And italic for emphasis or titles. Combined: bold italic.

Timeboxing. I gave myself a half-day. That constraint forced decisions. Bold as a lead-in introduces the point without shouting.

Highlight / Marker

Green highlighter-pen effect using <mark>. The gradient only colors the middle band (30-70%) of the text, mimicking a real highlighter. Used for inline emphasis — "this phrase matters."

The thing is, I'll see features that would be so useful, so close to what I need. But they don't address a broad enough audience's workflow, so they don't exist. Tools don't connect. I piece together workflows, but there's friction.

I asked Claude how to scope a proof of concept. It suggested two weeks. I ended up building it in two days. But the framework mattered.

Backstory

I've been working on this for about a year. Not continuously — it was one of those projects that lived in the back of my mind, occasionally surfacing during walks or late-night sessions.

The breakthrough came when I realized I was overthinking the technology and underthinking the documentation.

Footnotes

Superscript refs don't disrupt line height. Footnote section at bottom with smaller text.

The site uses a single HTML file1 with no build tools. This was a deliberate choice2 to keep iteration speed high.

  1. Inspired by the single-page approach used by many indie web projects.
  2. Build tools add complexity that slows down experimentation in the early stages.
Subsection Hierarchy

Instrument Serif italic — warm editorial contrast against the cold mono section labels. The three voices create the hierarchy: mono (label) → serif italic (subsection) → sans (body).

The Retro
Keep
  • Timeboxing. I gave myself a half-day. That constraint forced decisions.
  • Framing it as "transitional." I didn't have to get it perfect. I had to get it live.
  • Using voice memos and transcription to generate raw material. It lowers the friction of starting.
Drop
  • Deliberating about where, when, and why for too long before starting. I could have shipped months ago if I'd committed to iteration earlier.
Try
  • Documenting as I go, not after the fact. I regret not capturing more of the last year's experiments.
  • Testing AI visibility strategies on my own site. I've been implementing them for clients. Time to experiment here.
